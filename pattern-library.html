Parent-only pattern library

Real examples, handled carefully.

This section will collect real-world examples for parents: scams, fake proof, online grooming, rabbit holes, conspiracy thinking, wellness grifts, AI fakes, and information warfare. The point is not shock. The point is preparation.

Kid-facing lessons use recreated examples. Parent pattern files can be more direct, but they still avoid live links, tactical instructions, graphic detail, and anything kids should not be sent toward.

Two-sided by design

Questioning is a skill, not a team sport.

Some conspiracy claims are false. Some official stories have also been wrong, incomplete, or intentionally misleading. The library should help parents teach kids how to question well: what kind of claim is being made, what evidence would check it, how many people would have to coordinate a lie, and who benefits if the claim spreads.

Safety rules

What belongs here

Each entry should help parents understand a pattern and prepare a conversation. This page is a stub; no real pattern files have been added yet.

Use parent-safe summaries Explain what happened without reproducing harmful material.
Do not create rabbit-hole links No live links to extremist, predator, self-harm, or abuse communities.
Separate pattern from spectacle Focus on warning signs, recovery steps, and family scripts.

Pattern file template

A consistent structure for uncomfortable examples.

When we add real examples, this keeps the library useful instead of lurid. Every entry should end with what a parent can say and what a kid can practice.

Working title
Short, descriptive, non-sensational.
Risk area
Screenshot panic, grooming, rabbit hole, AI fake, grift, or information war.
What happened
Parent-safe summary with no graphic detail or tactical instructions.
Manipulation pattern
The repeatable tactic the kid should learn to recognize.
Good-question pattern
The careful question a kid or parent can ask without defaulting to cynicism or blind trust.
What kids should practice
One field check, one refusal script, or one ask-an-adult move.
What not to show kids
Any live source, graphic material, community names, or harmful tactical details.
Source type
Official resource, court document, platform report, or reputable journalism.
